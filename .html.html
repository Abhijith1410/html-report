02 · Data Protection

DPIA Requirements for Care Home Settings

Under UK GDPR (Article 35) and the Data Protection Act 2018, a DPIA is legally required before processing that is likely to result in high risk to individuals' rights and freedoms. Care homes meet this threshold by definition.

2.1 Legal Basis & Triggers

Article 35(1) of UK GDPR mandates a DPIA where processing — particularly using new technologies — is likely to result in high risk. The ICO identifies several criteria that, individually or in combination, trigger this obligation. Product X satisfies several simultaneously:

Mandatory Triggers Always Required
  • Processing special category health data on a large scale (Article 9 data)
  • Use of innovative technology in a health or social care setting
  • Processing personal data of vulnerable individuals (elderly residents)
  • Systematic processing for care needs assessment and resource allocation
  • Data sharing across multiple organisations (NHS, local authority)
Additional Considerations Context-Dependent
  • Biometric or behavioural monitoring features within Product X
  • Integration with GP Connect or Shared Care Records
  • Automated care plan suggestions or profiling
  • Staff monitoring or access-logging at scale
  • Significant changes to existing processing activities post-deployment
Key Principle: Under UK GDPR, a DPIA is a "live" document — it must be started before processing begins and updated whenever there is a significant change in how or why data is processed, the type of data used, or the technology involved in Product X deployments.

2.2 What a DPIA Must Document for Product X

The ICO's nine-step DPIA process requires systematic assessment of the following elements for each care home deployment:

  1. Identify the Need for a DPIA
    Confirm that Product X's processing of resident health records, medication data, care plans, and staff access logs meets mandatory triggers under Article 35. Document the screening decision.
  2. Describe the Processing
    Map all data flows: what data is collected (health status, ADL scores, medication), how it is stored, who has access (care staff, managers, NHS professionals), retention periods, and third-party processors.
  3. Assess Necessity & Proportionality
    Justify that data collected by Product X is the minimum necessary (data minimisation). Document lawful basis under Articles 6 and 9(2)(h) — processing necessary for health or social care provision.
  4. Identify & Assess Risks to Individuals
    Assess risks including unauthorised access to resident records, re-identification from anonymised data, discriminatory care decisions from algorithmic outputs, and third-party data processor failures.
  5. Identify Privacy Measures & Mitigations
    Document technical controls (encryption at rest/in transit, role-based access), organisational measures (staff training, DPO oversight), and contractual provisions with sub-processors.
  6. Sign Off & DPO Consultation
    The Data Protection Officer must be consulted throughout. If residual risk remains high after mitigation, consultation with the ICO is mandatory before deployment proceeds.
  7. Integrate Outcomes into the Project Plan
    Embed DPIA outputs into Product X's development, procurement, and deployment workflows. Privacy-by-design requirements must be translated into technical specifications.
  8. Record of Processing Activities (RoPA)
    Both the Product X vendor and each care home operator must maintain an Article 30 RoPA listing data categories, purposes, legal basis, recipients, and retention periods for all processing activities.
  9. Keep the DPIA Under Review
    Schedule regular reviews — at minimum annually and upon any significant change to Product X's functionality, data integrations, or the regulatory landscape (e.g., DUAA 2025 updates).

2.3 DPIA Obligations by Role

Care Home Operator (Data Controller)
  • Primary responsibility for completing and maintaining the DPIA
  • Must appoint a DPO if processing health data at scale
  • Publish privacy notices to residents, families, and staff
  • Ensure data processing agreements with Product X vendor
  • Submit DPIA to ICO if residual risk cannot be mitigated
Product X Vendor (Data Processor)
  • Provide controller with DPIA-ready technical documentation
  • Implement privacy-by-design into product architecture
  • Maintain data processing agreements with each operator
  • Notify controllers of any sub-processor changes
  • Support controller's DPIA process with system data flow maps

03 · Interoperability Standard

MODS Compliance — Scope & Requirements

The Minimum Operational Data Standard (MODS), published 1 April 2025 as information standard DAPB4102, defines a baseline of data that all CQC-registered care providers must collect in a standardised, interoperable format within their Digital Social Care Records.

3.1 Scope & Who It Affects

MODS was developed by the NHS England Digitising Social Care (DiSC) programme over four years, formally published April 2025, and applies to:

  • All CQC-registered adult social care providers using or preparing to use assured DSCRs
  • All DSCR assured solution suppliers — including vendors like Product X — on the NHS Assured Solutions List
  • All health IT system suppliers whose systems interface with the above providers
  • Applies across all care settings: care homes, supported living, home care
Deadline: All DSCR assured suppliers must comply by 1 July 2026. Non-compliance results in loss of NHS Assured status and removal from the approved solutions list.
Why MODS Was Needed

Before MODS, social care data was collected inconsistently — different labels, formats, and definitions across systems. This prevented meaningful data sharing between care providers and NHS partners such as GPs, hospitals, and integrated care systems.

MODS defines a common language for social care data — analogous to how GP Connect and Shared Care Records standardised data in the NHS. Once social care data is standardised, it can flow safely and meaningfully across organisations.

Standard Reference
DAPB4102
Published under Section 250, Health and Social Care Act 2012

3.3 Technical & Operational Requirements

Technical Requirements
  • Implement FHIR R4-aligned data structures for standardised data exchange
  • Adopt SNOMED CT clinical terminology for health conditions and care activities
  • Pass the MODS Assurance Tool — mandatory online validation before July 2026
  • Support NHS Login / NHS number as primary patient identifier across systems
  • Enable structured API-based data sharing with partner health systems
  • Ensure data portability so providers can switch DSCR without data loss
Operational Requirements
  • Train care staff to record data using standardised fields and terminology
  • Update care home workflows to align with MODS data collection requirements
  • Implement data quality processes to maintain accuracy of MODS-required fields
  • Establish information governance policies for sharing MODS data with NHS partners
  • Review and update consent and privacy notices to reflect new data flows
  • Coordinate with ICS/ICB partners on data sharing agreements for interoperability

3.4 Interoperability Benefits Once Compliant

Achieving MODS compliance unlocks several operational and strategic benefits for Product X and its care home customers:

NHS Integration

Standardised data enables seamless connection to GP systems, Shared Care Records, hospital discharge summaries, and the emerging Single Patient Record.

Safer Transitions of Care

Care professionals across settings can access consistent, structured data — reducing errors during hospital admissions, discharges, and care handovers.

Regulatory Confidence

CQC inspectors increasingly expect digital, standardised records. MODS-compliant records demonstrate good data governance and support quality ratings.

The two frameworks interact directly. MODS defines what data Product X must handle; DPIA governs how it must be protected. Together they shape every layer of the product — from data architecture to deployment contracts.

4.2 Privacy-by-Design Requirements Arising from MODS

MODS compliance expands the scope of data processing within Product X — which directly creates new DPIA obligations. Key integration points where both frameworks must align:

Data Minimisation vs. MODS Completeness: MODS requires comprehensive baseline data collection across all specified domains. UK GDPR requires data minimisation. Product X must demonstrate that all MODS-required fields are genuinely necessary for care delivery, and must not collect MODS fields that are not applicable to a specific resident's care context.
Interoperability & Consent: MODS enables data sharing with NHS systems. The DPIA must document the lawful basis for each sharing relationship — whether public task (Article 6(1)(e)), health care provision (Article 9(2)(h)), or another basis — and ensure residents are informed through updated privacy notices.
Data Quality as a Safety Issue: MODS-standardised data flows into NHS clinical decision-making. Inaccurate care records in Product X could directly harm patient safety. The DPIA must assess data quality risks and mandate validation controls within the product.

MODS Track

Immediate — May 2026
MODS Gap Analysis
Map all current Product X data fields against the MODS DAPB4102 specification published April 2025. Identify gaps in data definitions, missing mandatory fields, non-standardised code sets, and absent FHIR R4 structures. Register for the MODS Assurance Tool.

June 2026

Submit Product X for assessment through the mandatory MODS Assurance Tool operated by NHS England DiSC programme. Address any findings promptly. Confirm certification before the July 1 deadline. Communicate compliance status to existing care home customers.
1 July 2026 — DEADLINE
Full MODS Compliance Required
All DSCR assured suppliers must be fully MODS-compliant. Post-deadline: continue monitoring DiSC programme updates as MODS evolves. Engage with NHS systems for live interoperability testing. Begin NHS integration connections with ICS partners.

Conclusion: Product X operates at the intersection of two converging regulatory requirements. DPIA compliance under UK GDPR is an immediate legal obligation — processing of care home resident health data without a valid, DPO-reviewed DPIA carries live enforcement risk. MODS compliance is a time-bound commercial and regulatory imperative — the 1 July 2026 deadline is firm, and the consequences of non-compliance include loss of NHS Assured status and commercial exclusion.

The good news is that these frameworks are complementary, not contradictory. A well-constructed DPIA creates the governance architecture that makes MODS data flows lawful and auditable. MODS compliance creates the standardised, documented data infrastructure that supports DPIA risk mitigations. A unified compliance programme — led from the top, resourced appropriately, and treated as a strategic investment rather than a tick-box exercise — will position Product X as a trusted, interoperable, and privacy-respecting platform in the UK care home market.
